Only You Can Prevent Botnets

December 12, 2016
Jamie Harvey


Only you can prevent botnets!!  Botnets have been the talk of the industry lately.  DDOSing their way left and right, you have definitely heard of them by now.  What are botnets, why are they important, and how can they be stopped?

A botnet is a collection of *computers* that is connected to the internet, under the control of a person with malicious intentions.  The reason there are so many of these zombie computers is due to the explosion of the internet of things (IoT).  Manufacturers have identified a demand that more and more products be connected to the internet.  The manufacturers asked how to connect their device to the internet and nobody stopped to ask the question of why it needed to be connected in the first place.  So now we live in the age where your coffeemaker, fridge, microwave, TV, and many other devices are connected to the internet.

These devices rarely, if ever, receive security updates.  Sometimes they are created without any security concerns in the first place.  And other times, the software was created with malicious software to begin with!!  A number of new IoT devices are made quickly and cheap straight from China.  When I was in the military I became wary of equipment made by the lowest bidder (and that was lowest in the US).  Do we really want to trust things running on our network made by 8 cent per hour labor?  So the software running on your microwave gets compromised and joins an army of other compromised devices.  Then like an orchestra, the conductor begins and every device starts sending traffic to the targeted webservers.  This is what is known as a DDOS attack.  The webserver(s) become flooded with requests and packets.  It's like trying to drink water through a firehose with packets dropping left and right.

Most of these devices in your home connect through your router.  Yes, YOUR router is what is allowing these devices to become part of botnets that cripple DNS servers and prevent us good internet users from watching cat videos on our favorite websites.  As consumer connectivity and bandwidth improves, these attacks grow stronger and stronger.  The onerous falls on the consumer, as most ISPs tend to pass the buck.  Luckily, consumer grade routers have become pretty advanced.

Chances are you either have a firewall or a router that allows you to create firewall rules.  The most secure option is of course not connecting these devices to a network.  My parents recently got a smart TV that could connect to a network for some simple applications.  None of these applications would ever be used, so I asked them if they really wanted their new TV connected to the network.  They of course wanted it connected anyways.  So the next more secure option was to add the device, but create firewall rules to block all traffic to and from the device.  It showed up as connected, wouldn't become a botnet, and they were none the wiser.  Sometimes, blocking all traffic will break a service on the device.  Whether this service is critical or not is your call, and you'll need to use your networking skills to deduce what port/services are required for the service to work (hint: if the time stops working allow ntp).  Open up these ports/services and nothing more!

The next time a DDOS attack leaves your favorite website unreachable, make sure that you aren't part of the problem.
