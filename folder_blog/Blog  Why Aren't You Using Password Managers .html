Why Aren't You Using a Password Manager?

December 11, 2016
Jamie Harvey

Everyone knows that passwords are a pain to deal with.  If it's a strong password then it's impossible to remember.  And so many websites and systems require usernames and passwords.  It's very tempting to reuse passwords across a multitude of sites.  I know I've been tempted to.  Who can remember 10 different randomly generated passwords, all of which must be changed every 3 or 6 months?  Now who thinks anyone using the environment they administer can do the same?

But what if one of these accounts gets breached?  Most people tend to use the same or similar account names across a wide variety of services.  And the same with passwords.  So John Doe with e-mail jdoe@example.com uses the password johnpasswd for his email, Facebook, Google, Yahoo, banking website, and corporate Active Directory account.  Then he writes it down on a sticky note and leaves it on his computer monitor for all to see.

So the attacker gains access to John Doe's Facebook account.  He gets John's email address from Facebook and logs on to his email account.  John saved an email with instructions to access a VPN to his work's network.  What a surprise, his password is still johnpasswd and Mr. Hacker is sitting inside your network.

Enter the password manager.  A password manager is an application that creates and stores all of your usernames and passwords and hides them behind a master password.  The good ones will have applications for mobile and desktop systems that sync with each other.  I use 1Password.  It syncs encrypted keychains over Dropbox to backup my passwords.  My favorite feature for the Android mobile app is fingerprint authentication.  After authenticating inside the app, it has my saved logins with a title, username, password, url, and a note for a description.  When creating a new login, you can generate a password with letters, digits, and symbols with lengths from 4 to 50 characters.

With this app I have a different password for each computer system, network, web service.  This, with the help of 2 factor authentication (when available), will not only help prevent your accounts being hacked, but if one does get compromised it will be much harder (but not impossible) to lose any other accounts.

Have you educated your users on password management?  It's easy to forget that our environments contain many people who lack the computer skills we have in our industry.  No matter how hardened your environment may be, your users are an attack vector that can help or hurt your overall security.
